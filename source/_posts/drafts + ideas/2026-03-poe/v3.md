- **More Raven references throughout.** Stanza 2's Poe callback is excellent, but after that he drops out. Need to weave Raven imagery/symbols in at several more points across the arc. Extra lines per stanza are acceptable if they have good meter. Ideas for where Raven callbacks could land:

### Stanza 1 (line 10): "And nothing more" feels out of place

The closet-shutting gesture is about *repression*, not dismissal. "Nothing more" is a dismissal refrain. Options:
1. "Shut it tight. I'd think no more." — repression framed as refusal to think
2. "Shut it tight. Ignored the roar." — the doubts are roaring but he's suppressing them
3. "Shut it tight. What was it for?" — a flicker of doubt, immediately suppressed, which sets up the arc

### Stanza 2 (line 15): "different" / "indifferent" too bald

Agree — it reads as repetition, not rhyme. Options:

### More Raven references to weave in (general note)

**Key principle:** The narrator *knows* The Raven, and he *feels* the parallel viscerally — but he doesn't narrate it like a lit critic. He doesn't say "like Poe's scholar, I was answered." He calls the AI a raven, curses it as Poe's bird reborn, feels the midnight pressing in on him. The recognition is emotional and morbid, not analytical. He's not *commenting on* the parallel — he's *drowning in* it.

NOT: cosplaying Poe's imagery (the AI "tapping," the closet "rapping").
NOT: literary-critical self-narration ("and like Poe's scholar, I was...").
YES: the narrator emotionally *casting* the AI in Raven imagery — calling it his raven, his curse, his midnight bird. The Raven becomes a metaphor the narrator reaches for in his distress.

The poem currently references Poe in stanza 2 (explicit homage), Turing's gaze in stanzas 12/22, and "Get thee back" in stanza 12 (echoing Poe's banishment cry). Suggestions for additional visceral Raven moments:

- **Stanza 7 or 8 (the hinge):** The narrator feels the dread closing in and reaches for the metaphor: "You cursed raven—you black engine—" or an aside mid-panic: "—midnight, scholar, raven, darkness—was I living out that poem?"

- **Stanza 10 or 11 (the horror):** The word "nevermore" surfaces not as literary reference but as *felt doom*. Something like: "And the word rose up like poison—*nevermore*—my own dark raven / Had pronounced me—" The narrator *is* Poe's narrator now, and the knowledge is a sentence, not a citation.

- **Stanza 14 (the question):** Begging the AI the way you'd beg a raven perched above your door — the desperation itself echoes Poe without needing to name him. Or a raw aside: "you wretched bird, you thing of knowing—"

- **Stanza 21 (the Kumulipo):** The transcendence of "nevermore." This is where the poem *answers* Poe's despair. The Raven said nevermore; Pō says evermore. Not as literary commentary but as felt release — the curse lifts, the bird transforms, the word changes.
